# How to Identify a Manufactured Home Foundation Type

This is a field identification guide. It tells you how to figure out which type of foundation is under a manufactured home by looking at it — from the outside, and from underneath if you can get access. You do not need engineering training to use this guide. You do need a flashlight, a camera, and access to the perimeter of the home.

## Step 1: Look at the home from the outside

Stand back and look at the base of the home from each side. Ask yourself these questions:

**Can you see a gap between the bottom of the home and the ground?**

- If yes, and the gap is enclosed with lightweight panels (vinyl, metal, or similar material that is clearly not structural), you are probably looking at **skirting over a pier and anchor system**. Proceed to "Identifying pier and anchor systems" below.
- If yes, but the enclosure is a continuous masonry or concrete wall that looks structural, you are probably looking at a **crawl space foundation**. Proceed to "Identifying crawl space foundations" below.
- If no — the home appears to sit directly on or very close to a concrete surface at ground level — you are probably looking at a **slab-on-grade foundation**. Proceed to "Identifying slab-on-grade foundations" below.

**Can you see basement windows, a walkout door, or a full-height wall extending below the home on a sloped lot?**

- If yes, you are looking at a **basement foundation**. Proceed to "Identifying basement foundations" below.

**Is the home elevated significantly above grade (several feet) on what appear to be manufactured steel or concrete components that do not fit the descriptions above?**

- You may be looking at a **proprietary foundation system**. Look for manufacturer labels, branded components, or serial numbers. Proceed to "Identifying proprietary systems" below.

## Identifying pier and anchor systems

**Outside clues:**

- Skirting panels (vinyl, metal, concrete block veneer, or plywood) enclosing the area under the home — these panels are not structural and often have visible seams, fasteners, or gaps
- One or more access panels or vent openings in the skirting
- The home may sit 18 inches to several feet above grade
- You may see steel strap ends or anchor heads near the ground at the base of the skirting
- Wheels, axles, or a towing hitch may still be present (these should be removed for permanent foundation classification, but are often left in place on pier-and-anchor installations)

**Underneath clues (looking through access panel or where skirting is missing):**

- Two steel I-beams (the chassis) running the full length of the home
- Stacked concrete blocks sitting on flat concrete pads on the ground, spaced every 4 to 10 feet under the I-beams — these are the piers
- Or: pyramid-shaped steel jack stands with a threaded rod at the top, sitting on concrete pads
- Steel straps running diagonally from the I-beams down to the ground at an angle — these connect to auger anchors screwed into the soil
- Wooden shims or metal shims between the top of the pier and the bottom of the I-beam
- Ground surface may be bare soil, gravel, or covered with a polyethylene vapor barrier

**Red flags that indicate problems with a pier-and-anchor system:**

- Piers are visibly tilted, cracked, or have shifted off their pads
- Straps are loose, rusted through, or disconnected
- Pads are cracked or sinking into the soil
- Standing water under the home
- Single-stack concrete block piers taller than 36 inches (should be double-stacked). Piers over 67 inches require engineering per HUD code (24 CFR 3285.303(d)(1)(iv)); the FHA PFGMH allows prescriptive grouted/reinforced piers up to 80 inches as an alternative
- No pads under piers — blocks sitting directly on bare soil
- The home is visibly unlevel (doors don't close, floors slope)

## Identifying crawl space foundations

**Outside clues:**

- A continuous concrete, masonry, or wood-framed wall encloses the entire perimeter of the home at the base
- The wall looks structural — it is mortared concrete block, poured concrete, or a framed wall on a concrete stem
- The home appears to sit on this wall, not above it with a gap
- There may be small rectangular vent openings in the wall (these are crawlspace ventilation openings)
- There is typically a crawlspace access hatch or door somewhere in the perimeter wall
- The home has a "low profile" look, appearing close to the ground and similar to a site-built home
- No wheels, axles, or hitch visible (they must be removed for this type)

**Underneath clues (looking through crawlspace access):**

- The space under the home is enclosed by the perimeter wall on all sides
- The home's floor system sits on or is bolted to the top of the perimeter wall
- You can see anchor bolts or nailing strips connecting the sill or I-beams to the wall
- Interior support piers (concrete block columns, treated wood posts, or steel piers) support the chassis at interior span points
- A vapor barrier covers the ground
- The space is typically 18 inches to 3 feet in height (crawlable but not walkable)

**How to tell the difference between a crawl space and a pier system with masonry skirting:**

This is one of the most common sources of confusion. The key difference:
- **Crawl space:** The perimeter wall is structural. The home is physically attached to it (bolted, nailed, or mechanically fastened). The wall has a footing below it. If you push on the wall, it is solid and immovable.
- **Pier with masonry skirting:** The masonry at the perimeter is not attached to the home. It is a veneer or enclosure only. The home is still supported by interior piers and I-beams, and held down by ground anchors. If you look underneath, the weight is on the piers and I-beams, not on the perimeter wall.

**The test:** Look for anchor bolts or mechanical connections between the home and the top of the perimeter wall. If the home is bolted to the wall — it is a crawl space foundation. If the home sits on piers with straps to ground anchors, and the perimeter masonry is just skirting — it is a pier and anchor system, regardless of how finished the skirting looks.

## Identifying slab-on-grade foundations

**Outside clues:**

- The home sits at or very near ground level
- You can see a concrete edge at the base of the home — the slab extends slightly beyond the home footprint or is visible at the perimeter
- There is no crawlspace, no skirting, and no gap between the home and the ground
- The home appears to be bolted or bracketed to the concrete surface
- You may see anchor bolt heads or hold-down brackets at the home's perimeter where it meets the slab

**Perimeter clues (since you cannot get underneath):**

- The slab edge should be visible at the perimeter — look for a thickened edge or a turned-down footing
- Anchor bolts or brackets should be visible at the connection point between the home and the slab
- The grade around the slab should slope away from the home
- No standing water against the slab edge

**How to tell the difference between a slab and a pier system sitting on a concrete pad:**

- **Slab:** The concrete extends continuously under the entire home footprint. The home is anchored to it with embedded bolts or brackets. There is no accessible space underneath.
- **Pier on pad:** Individual concrete pads are under each pier, but the rest of the ground between piers is bare soil or gravel. There is still an accessible crawlspace (even if it is very shallow).

## Identifying basement foundations

**Outside clues:**

- On a sloped lot: a full-height wall extending below the home, often with windows, a door, or a garage opening at the lower level
- On a flat lot: the home appears elevated, with basement walls visible for a few inches to a few feet above grade, and typically window wells for below-grade basement windows
- The construction looks like a site-built basement — poured concrete or masonry walls, finished or unfinished

**Inside the basement:**

- Full-height (7-8 foot) enclosed space
- Concrete or masonry walls on all sides
- A poured concrete floor slab at the bottom
- Interior support columns (steel lally columns, masonry columns, or wood posts) supporting the beams above
- The manufactured home's chassis I-beams or floor joists visible at the ceiling of the basement
- Mechanical equipment (HVAC, water heater) may be located in the basement
- Anchor bolts or mechanical connections at the top of the walls where the home attaches

**How to tell the difference between a basement and a tall crawl space:**

- **Basement:** Habitable ceiling height (7+ feet). Poured concrete floor. May have finished walls, windows, doors, and mechanical equipment. The home was crane-set onto the walls.
- **Tall crawl space:** Less than habitable height (under 5-6 feet). May not have a poured floor slab. Not intended for habitation.

## Identifying proprietary systems

Proprietary systems are manufactured foundation products designed specifically for manufactured homes. They use engineered steel or concrete components and are identified by their branded nature.

**What to look for:**

- Manufactured steel frames, brackets, or anchoring devices that appear to be part of a designed system (not field-fabricated)
- Labels, stickers, or stamped markings with a company name, model number, or listing certification
- Components that are uniform, repeated, and appear factory-produced
- Installation that does not match any of the four non-proprietary descriptions above

**Common proprietary system names (if you see labels or documentation):**

- Oliver Technologies All Steel Foundation System — all-steel frame and pier system
- Fast Track Foundation Systems Anchorpanel Foundation — steel panel-based system
- JM Products Rigid Foundation Anchoring System — bracket and anchor-based system
- The Anchor Post Company Storm Anchor System — steel post and anchor system
- Tie Down Engineering Vector Dynamics — engineered frame system
- Tie Down Engineering Xi Foundation — engineered frame system

**What to do if you suspect a proprietary system:**

Do not attempt to evaluate it against generic pier-and-anchor rules. Instead, identify the manufacturer, obtain the manufacturer's installation instructions and engineering documentation, and compare the actual installation against those specific requirements.

## When you cannot determine the foundation type

If you cannot identify the foundation type from outside observation and limited access, this is itself an important finding. Common reasons include:

- Skirting completely encloses the perimeter with no access panel
- The underside is inaccessible due to low clearance, standing water, or unsafe conditions
- Components are hidden by fill, debris, or aftermarket modifications
- The system appears to be a hybrid (for example, pier and anchor with a partial perimeter wall)

In these cases, document what you can see, note what you cannot see and why, and escalate to a site visit or request additional evidence. Do not guess. An unclear foundation type is a valid and important finding.
