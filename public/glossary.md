# Glossary

## AHJ

Authority having jurisdiction. The local body that enforces permitting or code requirements for the manufactured home installation.

## Anchor assembly

Any device or means designed to transfer home anchoring loads to the ground. The most common type for manufactured homes is the auger-type (screw-in) ground anchor.

## Anchoring equipment

Ties, straps, cables, turnbuckles, chains, and other tensioning devices used to connect the manufactured home to anchor assemblies.

## Anchoring system

The complete combination of anchoring equipment and anchor assemblies that, when properly installed, resists uplift, overturning, and lateral forces on the home.

## Anchorage

The components and connections that resist uplift, sliding, or overturning. In the context of permanent foundations, rated anchorage capacity means the anchorage has been designed and tested for specific load values.

## Base flood elevation (BFE)

The elevation of the base flood (1% annual chance flood) including wave height, relative to the datum on the local flood hazard map. In flood hazard areas, the lowest floor of a manufactured home must be at or above the BFE.

## Blocking

Pieces of material (typically wood or metal) placed between the pier and the chassis I-beam to fill the gap and transfer the load. Distinguished from shimming by being thicker and serving a structural transfer function.

## Boxabl

Used in this repo as a shorthand for Boxabl-specific review paths that depend on the product configuration and manufacturer basis.

## Chassis

The permanent steel frame (I-beams, crossmembers, and outriggers) on which a manufactured home is built in the factory and transported to the site. The chassis remains part of the home after installation. In pier-and-anchor systems, the piers support the chassis. In crawl space and basement systems, the chassis may be recessed or may bear on the perimeter wall.

## CMU (concrete masonry unit)

A hollow-core concrete block used to build piers, perimeter walls, and other foundation components. The standard nominal size is 8x8x16 inches. When used as piers, CMUs are stacked with cells vertical.

## Crawl space

The accessible or partially accessible underfloor area below the home. In a crawl space foundation, this space is enclosed by a structural perimeter wall. Crawl spaces are not habitable and typically have 18 inches to 3 feet of clearance.

## DAPIA (Design Approval Primary Inspection Agency)

A state or private organization accepted by HUD that evaluates and approves manufactured home designs and quality control procedures.

## Data plate

A label inside the manufactured home that identifies the home's design specifications, including wind zone, roof load zone, thermal zone, and manufacturer information. Located in a utility closet, near the electrical panel, or in a bedroom closet.

## Efflorescence

White mineral deposits visible on the surface of concrete or masonry, caused by water migrating through the material and depositing dissolved salts as it evaporates. In foundation inspection, efflorescence is an indicator of chronic moisture migration through walls or slabs.

## Double-stack pier

A concrete block pier constructed with two blocks side by side in an interlocking pattern. Required by 24 CFR 3285.303 when block pier height exceeds 36 inches, up to 80 inches maximum.

## FHA (Federal Housing Administration)

The Federal Housing Administration, a division of HUD. FHA insures mortgages, including those on manufactured homes. The FHA permanent foundation definition (from the PFGMH) is the standard used to determine whether a manufactured home on a permanent foundation qualifies for FHA-insured financing.

## Flood hazard area

The area shown on the FEMA Flood Insurance Rate Map (FIRM) as subject to a 1% annual chance flood (100-year flood). Manufacturing homes in these areas must meet special foundation and elevation requirements per 24 CFR 3285.102.

## Footing

The portion of the support system that transmits loads directly to the soil. For piers, the footing is typically a concrete pad at the base. For perimeter walls, the footing is a continuous concrete strip below the wall. The PFGMH requires footings to be reinforced concrete to qualify as permanent.

## Foundation system

A system of support capable of transferring all design loads to the ground, including all elements of the support system. Per HUD, this includes piers, footings, slabs, walls, ties, anchoring equipment, ground anchors, and any other material that supports and secures the home.

## FPSF (frost-protected shallow foundation)

A foundation design that uses perimeter insulation instead of deep footings to prevent frost heave. Designed per SEI/ASCE 32-01 and permitted by 24 CFR 3285.312.

## Frost depth (frost line)

The maximum depth in the ground to which water in the soil freezes. Footings must be placed below this depth to prevent frost heave. Varies from 0 inches in the deep south to 100+ inches in northern states and Alaska.

## Ground anchor

A specific anchoring assembly device designed to transfer home anchoring loads to the ground. The most common type is the auger (screw-in) anchor, a steel shaft with a helix plate screwed into the soil.

## HUD

The U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development. HUD standards and guidance are central to manufactured home installation, construction, and lending requirements.

## HUD label

A metal certification label affixed to the exterior of each transportable section of a manufactured home, indicating that the home was built to the Federal Manufactured Home Construction and Safety Standards (24 CFR Part 3280). Located on the tail-light end of each section.

## I-beam

The main structural steel beams that form the longitudinal frame of the manufactured home chassis. Piers and supports bear under these beams. Straps connect over these beams to ground anchors.

## Lally column

A steel pipe column, typically filled with concrete, used as an interior support post in basements and crawl spaces. Lally columns carry beam loads down to the floor slab or footing below.

## Marriage line (mating line)

The junction where two sections of a multi-section (double-wide or triple-wide) manufactured home are joined. Additional piers or supports are typically required along the marriage line.

## MHCSS (Manufactured Home Construction and Safety Standards)

The federal building code for manufactured homes, established in 24 CFR Part 3280. Covers structural design, fire safety, plumbing, mechanical, and electrical requirements.

## Manufactured home

A structure transportable in one or more sections, 8 feet or more in width or 40 feet or more in length when erected on site, or 320+ square feet, built on a permanent chassis to HUD code, and designed as a dwelling. Distinguished from modular homes (built to state/local code) and mobile homes (pre-1976).

## Pad (footing pad)

A flat concrete surface placed on the ground under a pier to spread the pier load over a larger area of soil. May be precast or poured in place.

## Perimeter support pier

A pier located under the outer edge of the manufactured home floor system or perimeter wall, as distinguished from interior (frame/chassis) piers.

## Permanent foundation

As defined by the FHA/HUD: a foundation constructed of durable materials (concrete, mortared masonry, or treated wood), site-built, with rated anchorage, reinforced concrete footings below frost depth, and a continuous perimeter wall. Screw-in soil anchors are not considered permanent anchorage.

## PFGMH

Permanent Foundations Guide for Manufactured Housing (HUD-007487, September 1996). The standard reference for determining whether a manufactured home foundation meets permanent-foundation requirements for FHA/HUD lending purposes.

## Pier

The portion of the support system between the footing and the manufactured home, exclusive of shims. Types include manufactured steel stands, pressure-treated wood, concrete blocks, and manufactured concrete piers.

## Proprietary foundation system

A manufactured foundation product owned and produced by a single company, using engineered components tested and listed by a nationally recognized testing agency. Distinguished from non-proprietary systems that use commonly available materials.

## Remote review

A desk-based engineering review using submitted evidence (photos, appraisals, plans) instead of an in-person site visit.

## Shim

Thin material placed between the top of a pier and the bottom of the chassis I-beam to achieve level support. Should be durable material (hardwood, metal, or approved material), not softwood scraps.

## Site tech

Field personnel sent to document conditions that are too unclear for a conservative remote-only conclusion.

## Soil bearing capacity

The maximum pressure the soil can support without excessive settlement or failure, measured in pounds per square foot (psf). 24 CFR 3285.202 provides a classification table (Classes 1 through 5) ranging from 4,000+ psf for rock to near-zero for peat. If the soil type is unknown, a default of 1,500 psf is permitted unless site conditions indicate otherwise.

## Skirting

Weather-resistant material (vinyl, metal, concrete block veneer, or other) used to enclose the perimeter under a manufactured home, from the bottom of the home to grade. Skirting is non-structural in pier-and-anchor systems. In crawl space foundations, the enclosure is a structural perimeter wall, not skirting.

## Stabilizer plate

A metal plate used at the head of a ground anchor to reduce movement and improve the anchor's resistance to loads.

## Strap (tie strap)

A steel band connecting the manufactured home's chassis to a ground anchor. Diagonal straps (from I-beam to ground anchor) resist uplift. Vertical straps (from sidewall to ground anchor) are required in Wind Zones II and III.

## Support system

Pilings, columns, footings, piers, foundation walls, shims, and any combination thereof that, when properly installed, support the manufactured home.

## Vapor barrier (vapor retarder)

A moisture-resistant material (minimum 6-mil polyethylene) placed on the ground under the home to reduce moisture migration into the crawlspace. Required when the area under the home is enclosed, except in arid regions (24 CFR 3285.204).

## Wind zone

Geographic areas designated on the HUD Wind Zone Map that determine the wind design loads for manufactured homes. Zone I covers most of the interior US. Zone II and Zone III cover hurricane-prone coastal areas and require enhanced anchorage.
