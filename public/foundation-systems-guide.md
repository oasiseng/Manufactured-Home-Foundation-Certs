# Manufactured Home Foundation Systems Guide

This guide is a public-facing overview of foundation systems commonly used for manufactured homes and similar light modular installations.

## Core idea

There is no single best foundation system. The right answer depends on the site, the structure, the governing code path, and the performance objective.

A foundation system must do three things well:

- support the structure
- transfer loads into the ground
- resist uplift, sliding, and overturning

## Common system types

### Pier and anchor systems

Often the fastest and lowest-cost installation path. These systems are common when budget and installation speed matter, but they may not satisfy every financing or permanent-foundation requirement.

### Crawl space foundations

Perimeter support with accessible underfloor space. These systems can improve aesthetics, support utility access, and align better with real-property lending expectations when designed and built properly.

### Slab-on-grade foundations

Useful when the site and structural layout support a direct concrete bearing strategy. Slab-on-grade systems can perform well when moisture, drainage, and soil behavior are managed correctly.

### Basement foundations

The highest-cost option, but they can offer usable space and a strong permanent-installation profile in colder climates or higher-end projects.

### Proprietary or manufacturer-driven systems

Some products rely on manufacturer-specific details, pre-engineered packages, or limited foundation families. These cases should be reviewed against the actual product basis rather than generic assumptions.

## Design drivers

- soil bearing capacity
- frost depth
- flood exposure
- wind and uplift demand
- seismic demand where applicable
- drainage and moisture control
- compatibility with the chassis or module support system
- local code and approval path

## Best practices

- Verify manufacturer or installer requirements before assuming a generic detail applies.
- Follow the actual load path, not just the visible skirting.
- Document accessory structures clearly, especially when decks, porches, or stairs are attached.
- Do not rely on poor drainage or unsupported fill to perform like engineered support.
- Treat site-specific issues conservatively when evidence is weak.

## Disclaimer

This guide is educational only. Final conclusions should be based on the actual site, the actual structure, the governing requirements, and licensed engineering judgment where required.
