# Manufactured Home Foundation Systems Guide

This guide explains the foundation systems used under HUD-code manufactured homes. It is written for homeowners, appraisers, loan processors, and field inspectors who need to identify what they are looking at and understand what matters for certification, lending, and permanent-foundation compliance.

## Why foundation type matters

The foundation system under a manufactured home determines whether the home can be financed as real property, whether it meets HUD and FHA permanent-foundation requirements, and whether it is adequately supported for the life of the structure. Getting the type wrong, or failing to notice a deficiency, can stall a loan, void insurance eligibility, or mask a safety problem.

The HUD Permanent Foundations Guide for Manufactured Housing (PFGMH) and 24 CFR Part 3285 (Model Manufactured Home Installation Standards) define the rules. This guide translates those rules into plain language.

## The four non-proprietary foundation classifications

HUD and the PFGMH organize manufactured home foundations into four non-proprietary categories. Each one has a different physical appearance, different cost profile, and different implications for real-property classification and permanent-foundation compliance.

---

### 1. Pier and anchor systems

This is the most common foundation type for manufactured homes in the United States. It is also the one most likely to cause confusion during lending and certification, because whether it qualifies as a permanent foundation depends on the specific anchorage method and perimeter enclosure — not the piers themselves.

**What it looks like from outside the home.** The home sits above grade on visible supports. You will typically see skirting (vinyl, metal, concrete block, or other material) enclosing the area between the bottom of the home and the ground. The skirting is not structural — it is weather protection and aesthetics. If skirting is removed or damaged, you can see underneath the home to the pier and anchor system directly.

**What it looks like underneath the home.** When you look under the home (through a skirting access panel or where skirting is missing), you will see:

- Steel I-beams running the length of the home (the chassis or main beams). These are the original transport frame the home was built on.
- Concrete block piers or steel jack stands placed at intervals under the I-beams. These carry the weight of the home down to the ground.
- Square concrete pads or footings under each pier, spreading the load onto the soil.
- Steel straps wrapped over the I-beams and connected down to ground anchors (auger-type devices screwed into the soil). These resist wind uplift.
- For multi-section (double-wide) homes, additional piers along the marriage line (where the two halves meet) and possibly along perimeter walls.

**Pier types you will see.** The most common pier types are hollow-core concrete masonry blocks (CMU) stacked vertically, and pyramid-shaped steel jack stands with an adjustable rod at the top. Single-stack block piers should not exceed 36 inches in height. Block piers over 36 inches and up to 80 inches must be double-stacked (two blocks side by side in an interlocking pattern). Piers over 80 inches require engineering design. You may also see pressure-treated wood posts or manufactured metal/concrete piers that are listed and labeled for specific load capacities.

**Anchor types you will see.** The standard anchor is an auger-type (screw-in) ground anchor — a steel shaft with a helix plate at the bottom, screwed into the soil. Steel straps connect the anchor head to the home's I-beams. In Wind Zones II and III (Gulf Coast, Atlantic Coast, Hawaii), you will also see vertical straps from the sidewall of the home down to ground anchors, in addition to the diagonal straps from the I-beams. Stabilizer plates at the anchor head reduce movement. The spacing of anchors and straps is dictated by the home manufacturer's installation instructions and the wind zone.

**Real property classification.** Pier and block systems can qualify as permanent foundations when the installation meets the FHA/PFGMH criteria. The piers themselves — CMU blocks, concrete, treated wood — are durable materials and are not the issue. The critical questions are: (1) Is the anchorage rated and permanent? Screw-in soil anchors alone are specifically excluded from the permanent anchorage definition. Anchors embedded in concrete or other engineered connections can meet the requirement. (2) Are the footings reinforced concrete and below frost depth? (3) Is there a continuous perimeter enclosure wall separating the crawl space from the surrounding soil and keeping out vermin and water? Many pier and block installations are certified as permanent foundations when they include reinforced concrete footings, rated anchorage, and an appropriate perimeter enclosure. The determination depends on the specific installation, not on the foundation category alone.

**What an inspector must verify for this type:**

- Pier material: concrete block, steel jack stands, treated wood, or manufactured piers — and whether piers are listed/labeled if manufactured
- Pier height: single-stack block piers should be 36 inches or less; double-stack required above 36 inches; engineering required above 80 inches
- Pier spacing: typically 4 to 10 feet on center, must be consistent with manufacturer installation instructions and 24 CFR 3285.303 Table 1, 2, or 3
- Pier condition: plumb, no visible tilt, rotation, cracking, or displacement
- Footing presence and material: pads or footers under each pier, adequate size for soil bearing capacity (minimum 1,500 psf default per 24 CFR 3285.202)
- Anchor presence and type: auger anchors, steel straps, stabilizer plates
- Strap condition: tight, no visible corrosion or slack
- Shim condition: shims between the pier top and the I-beam should be durable material (not stacked wood scraps)
- Leveling: no more than 1/4 inch difference between adjacent pier supports (24 CFR 3285.6)
- Wheels, axles, and towing hitch: confirm whether removed or still present
- Vapor barrier: 6-mil polyethylene on the ground under the home (required if enclosed with skirting, except in arid regions per 24 CFR 3285.204)
- Ventilation: adequate crawlspace ventilation openings in skirting or perimeter

### 2. Crawl space foundations

Crawl space foundations are designed for real-property classification. They use a structural perimeter wall to support the home and enclose an accessible (but not habitable) space underneath. This is the most common permanent-foundation approach for manufactured homes that need to qualify for conventional or FHA financing.

**What it looks like from outside the home.** The home appears to sit on a continuous perimeter wall, similar to a site-built home. The wall extends from grade down to a footing below the frost line. You will see a finished exterior that may be poured concrete, concrete block (CMU), or a wood-framed pony wall on a concrete stem wall. There is typically minimal visible gap between the bottom of the home and the top of the wall. The overall profile can look very similar to a site-built home, especially in a "low-profile" installation where the home sits close to grade.

**What it looks like underneath the home.** Access is through a crawlspace access opening (hatch or door in the perimeter wall). Inside you will see:

- The perimeter foundation wall enclosing the entire underside
- The home's floor joists bearing directly on the perimeter wall via a sill plate, or the chassis I-beams resting on the wall with the floor system above
- Interior piers (concrete block columns, treated wood posts, or steel piers) supporting the chassis I-beams at interior locations
- For multi-section homes, ridge beam support piers along the marriage line
- Anchor bolts connecting the sill or the I-beams to the top of the perimeter wall
- A vapor barrier (6-mil polyethylene) on the ground
- Ventilation openings in the perimeter walls
- Footing at the base of the perimeter wall, extending below the local frost depth

**Key difference from pier-and-anchor systems.** The home is mechanically fastened to the perimeter wall (bolted, nailed with approved nailing strips, or welded). The perimeter wall itself is the structural and lateral load path — no separate ground anchors are needed. The wall resists uplift, overturning, and sliding through its attachment to the footing and the weight of the soil and structure.

**Construction variations you will encounter.** The perimeter wall may be poured concrete, mortared and grouted concrete block, or a wood-framed pony wall on a concrete or block stem wall. Some designs use a combination — for example, a concrete stem wall below grade with a framed pony wall above. Interior supports range from economical treated wood posts to block columns to manufactured steel piers.

**Real property classification.** A properly constructed crawl space foundation typically qualifies as a permanent foundation under the FHA definition, because it meets the core requirements: durable materials, site-built, rated anchorage through the perimeter wall attachment, reinforced concrete footings below frost depth, and a continuous wall enclosing the space.

**What an inspector must verify for this type:**

- Perimeter wall material and construction: poured concrete, grouted CMU, pony wall on stem wall — and whether it is continuous around the full perimeter
- Footing: present below the perimeter wall, below local frost depth, reinforced concrete
- Wall-to-footing connection: adequate reinforcement, no visible cracking, displacement, or separation
- Home-to-wall attachment: anchor bolts, approved nailing strips, or equivalent mechanical fastening — confirm that the home is structurally connected, not just resting on the wall
- Interior pier supports: material, spacing, condition, and footing under each interior pier
- Wheels, axles, and towing hitch: must be removed for permanent foundation classification
- Vapor barrier: 6-mil polyethylene on crawlspace floor
- Ventilation: openings in perimeter wall adequate per code or manufacturer instructions
- Drainage: grade slopes away from foundation, no standing water or signs of ponding in crawlspace
- Clearance: minimum clearance under the home for access and inspection (18 inches typical minimum)
- Signs of distress: cracking in walls or footings, settlement, tilt, moisture damage, wood rot on sills or posts

### 3. Slab-on-grade foundations

Slab foundations place the manufactured home on a poured concrete slab that serves as both the footing and the floor-level support surface. These are common in warmer climates and in areas with expansive soils where a slab can move uniformly with the soil.

**What it looks like from outside the home.** The home sits directly on or very close to the concrete slab. In some designs the slab is visible at the perimeter as a concrete edge. The home may appear to sit almost at ground level. There is no crawlspace and typically no skirting, because the slab closes the gap. In some installations the home's chassis I-beams rest on the slab surface with blocking; in others the I-beams are recessed into channels formed in the slab.

**What it looks like at the foundation level.** Because there is no accessible crawlspace, the inspector sees the slab from the perimeter and any accessible edges:

- A concrete slab, typically 4 to 6 inches thick, with a thickened edge or turned-down footing at the perimeter
- Anchor bolts or hold-down brackets embedded in the slab, connecting the home's chassis or floor system to the concrete
- Interior support points may be thickened areas in the slab (grade beams) where the chassis I-beams rest
- A vapor barrier under the slab (not visible after construction, but required)
- Drainage provisions around the slab perimeter

**Key difference from crawl space.** There is no accessible underside to inspect. All support and anchorage must be verified from the perimeter, from the slab edge, or from construction documentation. This makes remote review more difficult and site visits more valuable.

**Construction variations.** The slab may be a monolithic pour (slab and footing cast together) or a two-pour system (footing first, then slab). In frost-prone areas, the slab perimeter may include insulation and frost-protected shallow foundation (FPSF) design per SEI/ASCE 32-01, or the footing may extend below the frost line at the edges. Some designs use a "floating" slab that moves with the soil and does not extend below the frost line — these are generally limited to areas with minimal frost.

**Real property classification.** A slab-on-grade foundation typically qualifies as a permanent foundation when it includes reinforced concrete, rated anchorage (embedded anchor bolts or brackets), adequate footing depth or frost protection, and a continuous perimeter support.

**What an inspector must verify for this type:**

- Slab condition: no significant cracking, heaving, settling, or displacement visible at the perimeter or edges
- Anchorage: anchor bolts or hold-down brackets connecting the home to the slab — confirm type, spacing, and tightness
- Perimeter footing: thickened edge or turned-down footing at the slab perimeter, below frost depth where required
- Home-to-slab connection: the home must be mechanically attached, not just resting on the slab
- Wheels, axles, and towing hitch: must be removed
- Drainage: grade slopes away from the slab in all directions (minimum 1/2 inch per foot for the first 10 feet per 24 CFR 3285.203)
- Moisture: no signs of water intrusion, ponding, or undermining at the slab edge
- Slab reinforcement: if construction documents are available, confirm reinforcement (rebar or wire mesh)
- Expansion joints: present and in acceptable condition if the slab is large
- Utility penetrations: clean entries through or under the slab, no settlement around penetrations

### 4. Basement foundations

Basement foundations are the highest-cost option but provide usable space below the manufactured home. These are most common in colder climates where deep frost lines require excavation below grade anyway, and in higher-end manufactured home installations.

**What it looks like from outside the home.** The home sits on what appears to be a full basement, similar to a site-built home. The basement walls extend from the footing at the bottom of the excavation up to the floor level of the manufactured home. On a sloped lot, one or more walls may be partially or fully above grade (a walkout or daylight basement). On a flat lot, the basement walls may be almost entirely below grade with only a few inches visible.

**What it looks like inside the basement.** The basement is a fully enclosed space with:

- Concrete or masonry foundation walls forming the perimeter
- A concrete floor slab at the bottom
- The manufactured home's floor system supported on the top of the basement walls
- Interior support columns (steel posts, masonry columns, or wood posts) carrying the chassis I-beams or ridge beams where the span exceeds what the floor system can handle unsupported
- The I-beams or chassis may be visible or may be enclosed in the basement ceiling framing
- Anchor bolts or mechanical connections fastening the home to the top of the basement walls

**Key differences from crawl space.** The space is habitable height (typically 7-8 feet of clear headroom). The walls bear against soil pressure on the outside and must be designed as retaining walls. The footing system is larger and deeper. The home delivery usually requires a crane to set the home on top of the basement walls, because there is no way to drive the home into position.

**Construction variations.** Basement walls may be poured concrete, grouted concrete block, or insulated concrete forms (ICFs). Some designs use a modified floor/chassis system where the steel chassis is recessed so that the floor joists bear directly on the basement walls, eliminating the forest of interior columns that would otherwise be needed. The home manufacturer typically must provide a "foundation-ready" chassis for this configuration.

**Real property classification.** A basement foundation inherently qualifies as a permanent foundation under the FHA definition, because it is site-built from durable materials with deep footings and a continuous enclosure wall. However, basements are specifically excluded from the National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP) in flood hazard areas because they create below-grade living space.

**What an inspector must verify for this type:**

- Wall material and construction: poured concrete, grouted CMU, or ICFs — confirm structural adequacy
- Footing: continuous reinforced concrete footing under the basement walls, below frost depth
- Wall condition: no significant cracking, bowing, or displacement — especially horizontal cracking in block walls (sign of lateral soil pressure failure)
- Home-to-wall connection: anchor bolts or mechanical fastening at the top of the walls, connecting the home's sill, I-beam, or floor system to the foundation
- Interior support columns: material, spacing, condition, and connection to the floor slab and to the beams above
- Floor slab: poured concrete, adequate thickness, no significant cracking or heaving
- Waterproofing: no signs of water intrusion through walls or floor slab, no efflorescence (white mineral deposits indicating water migration)
- Drainage: exterior perimeter drain tile or French drain system, functioning sump pump if applicable
- Wheels, axles, and towing hitch: must be removed
- Backfill condition: no settlement of soil against the basement walls, no exposed foundation wall that should be below grade
- Egress: if the basement is habitable or finished, confirm emergency egress windows or doors per code

## Proprietary foundation systems

In addition to the four non-proprietary types above, several companies manufacture proprietary foundation systems designed specifically for manufactured homes. These include products from companies such as Oliver Technologies (All Steel Foundation System), Fast Track Foundation Systems (Anchorpanel Foundation), JM Products (Rigid Foundation Anchoring System), The Anchor Post Company (Storm Anchor System), and Tie Down Engineering (Vector Dynamics and Xi Foundation systems).

Proprietary systems are typically engineered, tested, and listed by a nationally recognized testing agency. They use manufactured components (steel frames, brackets, anchoring devices) that are installed according to the manufacturer's specific instructions. Most proprietary systems are designed to qualify as permanent foundations and to work in seismic, flood, and frost-heave areas.

**What to look for with proprietary systems.** If you see manufactured steel components, branded brackets, or a system that does not fit neatly into one of the four non-proprietary categories above, it may be a proprietary system. The key identification step is to look for manufacturer labels, model numbers, or branded components. The review should then be compared against the specific manufacturer's installation instructions and engineering basis — not against generic pier-and-anchor assumptions.

**What an inspector must verify for proprietary systems:**

- Identify the manufacturer and system name from labels, documentation, or visual identification
- Confirm installation matches the manufacturer's instructions and engineering drawings
- Verify that the system is listed or certified by a nationally recognized testing agency
- Check anchorage connections, bolt torques, and component condition per the manufacturer's specification
- Do not substitute generic pier-and-anchor rules for a proprietary system's specific requirements

## The permanent foundation question

The single most common question in manufactured home foundation certification is: "Does this foundation qualify as a permanent foundation?" The answer depends on the FHA/HUD definition, which requires:

**Vertical stability requirements:**

1. Rated anchorage capacity to prevent uplift and overturning due to wind or seismic forces (whichever controls). Screw-in soil anchors are NOT considered permanent anchorage.
2. Footing size adequate to prevent overloading the soil-bearing capacity and avoid settlement. Footings must be reinforced concrete to be considered permanent.
3. Base of footing below the maximum frost penetration depth.
4. A continuous wall (bearing or non-bearing) enclosing the basement or crawl space, separating it from backfill, and keeping out vermin and water.

**Lateral stability requirements:**

5. Rated anchorage capacity to prevent sliding due to wind or seismic forces, in both the transverse and longitudinal directions.

In practical terms, the determining factors are the anchorage method, footing design, and perimeter enclosure — not the foundation category name. Pier and block systems with rated anchorage (not screw-in anchors alone), reinforced concrete footings below frost depth, and a continuous perimeter enclosure wall can qualify. Systems relying solely on screw-in soil anchors for uplift resistance do not meet the permanent anchorage requirement. Crawl space, slab-on-grade, and basement foundations generally qualify when properly designed and constructed. Proprietary systems qualify when they are listed and installed per manufacturer instructions.

## Design factors that affect every foundation type

Regardless of which system is installed, several site and environmental conditions influence foundation design and must be evaluated:

**Soil.** The soil classification and bearing capacity must be determined before the foundation is built (24 CFR 3285.202). If the soil type can be identified, the bearing capacity can be estimated from the HUD soil classification table (Classes 1 through 5, ranging from 4,000+ psf for rock to near-zero for peat). If unknown, a default of 1,500 psf is permitted unless site conditions indicate otherwise. The foundation must be built on firm, undisturbed soil or fill compacted to at least 90% of maximum density. Organic material must be removed.

**Frost depth.** In areas where the ground freezes, footings must extend below the frost line to prevent heaving. Frost depths range from near zero in the southern states to 100 inches in parts of the northern US and Alaska. The local building authority determines the required frost depth. An alternative to deep footings is a frost-protected shallow foundation (FPSF) using perimeter insulation.

**Flood hazard areas.** In FEMA-designated flood hazard areas, the lowest floor must be at or above the Base Flood Elevation (BFE). The foundation must resist flotation, collapse, and lateral movement under flood and wind loads. Basements are excluded from NFIP eligibility. Pier-and-anchor, crawl space, and slab systems can be adapted for flood areas when properly engineered.

**Wind zones.** HUD defines three wind zones (I, II, and III) that dictate anchorage requirements. Zone I covers most of the interior US. Zones II and III cover hurricane-prone coastal areas. Higher wind zones require more anchors, closer spacing, and additional sidewall straps. The home's data plate indicates the wind zone it was designed for.

**Seismic risk.** Foundation systems in seismic areas must be designed to resist lateral forces. The HUD code wind provisions generally provide adequate seismic resistance, but high seismic zones may require additional engineering.

**Drainage.** All drainage must slope away from the home at a minimum of 1/2 inch per foot for the first 10 feet (24 CFR 3285.203). Standing water under or around the foundation causes erosion, settlement, and moisture damage.

## Disclaimer

This guide is educational only. It summarizes the HUD Model Installation Standards (24 CFR Part 3285) and the HUD Permanent Foundations Guide for Manufactured Housing for general understanding. Final determinations about foundation adequacy, permanent-foundation classification, and real-property status must be based on the actual site conditions, the actual structure, the governing requirements of the authority having jurisdiction, and the professional judgment of a licensed engineer.
