# Site-Tech Checklist

Use this checklist when a case escalates to an in-person verification path.

## Before the visit

- Review the intake package and remote-review notes.
- Confirm the exact questions the engineer needs answered.
- Confirm access instructions, occupancy status, and safety constraints.
- Review the preliminary foundation type classification from intake, if one was made.
- Bring camera, flashlight, measuring tools, PPE, and note template.

## On site — universal observations

- Verify address and structure identity.
- Confirm the structure is a manufactured home (HUD label, data plate, or chassis visible).
- Photograph all elevations and corners from far enough back to show grade and foundation.
- Photograph the transition from grade to foundation/skirting on each side.
- Note drainage, slope, erosion, flood exposure cues, or undermining on all sides.
- Record areas that remain inaccessible and why.
- Confirm whether wheels, axles, and towing hitch have been removed.
- Document all additions and whether they appear structurally independent or integrated.
- Record the leveling condition: do doors/windows appear to operate, is visible slope present.

## On site — foundation type identification

Before proceeding to type-specific observations, identify the foundation type:

- Is the perimeter enclosed with non-structural skirting (vinyl, metal panels, etc.)? → likely **pier and anchor**
- Is the perimeter enclosed with a continuous structural wall (poured concrete, grouted CMU, or framed on a stem wall)? → likely **crawl space**
- Does the home sit directly on a concrete slab with no accessible underside? → likely **slab-on-grade**
- Is there a full-height below-grade space with habitable ceiling height? → likely **basement**
- Are manufactured/branded steel components visible that do not fit the above categories? → likely **proprietary system**

If the foundation type is unclear, document what you see and note it as ambiguous. Do not force a classification.

## Type-specific observations: pier and anchor

- Photograph support system at multiple locations under the home.
- Identify pier material: concrete block (CMU), steel jack stands, treated wood, or manufactured piers.
- Note pier height: are single-stack block piers 36 inches or less? If taller, are they double-stacked?
- Note pier spacing (estimate or measure at representative locations).
- Photograph pier condition: plumb, intact, or tilted/cracked/displaced.
- Photograph footings/pads under piers: material, approximate size, condition.
- Photograph shims between pier tops and I-beams.
- Identify anchor type: auger ground anchors, steel straps, stabilizer plates.
- Photograph strap connections at I-beams and at anchor heads — note tension/slack.
- In Wind Zones II/III: look for vertical sidewall straps in addition to diagonal I-beam straps.
- Note vapor barrier presence and condition.
- Note ventilation openings in skirting.

## Type-specific observations: crawl space

- Access the crawlspace through the perimeter wall hatch/door.
- Identify perimeter wall material: poured concrete, grouted CMU, pony wall on stem wall.
- Confirm the wall is continuous around the full perimeter (look for gaps or breaks).
- Photograph the home-to-wall connection: look for anchor bolts through a sill plate, nailing strips, or bracket connections.
- Photograph interior support piers: material, spacing, condition, and footings under each.
- Note whether the chassis I-beams rest on the wall or whether the floor joists bear directly on the wall.
- Note crawlspace clearance (estimate height at lowest point).
- Note vapor barrier presence and condition.
- Note ventilation openings in perimeter wall.
- Look for signs of moisture: standing water, efflorescence on walls, wood rot on sills or posts.
- Look for cracking in the perimeter wall — especially horizontal cracking in block walls.
- Photograph the footing where visible at the base of the perimeter wall.

## Type-specific observations: slab-on-grade

- Photograph the slab edge on all sides where visible.
- Look for a thickened edge or turned-down footing at the perimeter.
- Photograph anchor bolts or hold-down brackets connecting the home to the slab.
- Note bolt spacing and condition (tight, loose, corroded).
- Look for slab cracking, heaving, or settlement at the visible edges.
- Note grade and drainage around the slab perimeter.
- Look for moisture or water damage at the slab edge.
- Note that the underside is not accessible — document this limitation.
- Photograph utility penetrations through the slab.

## Type-specific observations: basement

- Enter the basement and photograph all four walls.
- Identify wall material: poured concrete, grouted CMU, or ICFs.
- Look for horizontal or step cracking in walls (sign of lateral soil pressure).
- Look for bowing or inward displacement of walls.
- Photograph the home-to-wall connection at the top of the walls.
- Photograph interior support columns: material, connection to floor slab and beams above.
- Note the basement floor slab condition: cracking, heaving, settlement.
- Look for water intrusion: staining, efflorescence, active leaks, sump pump condition.
- Note backfill condition from the outside: settlement, exposed wall that should be below grade.
- If the basement is habitable/finished, note egress provisions.

## Type-specific observations: proprietary systems

- Look for manufacturer labels, model numbers, or branded components.
- Photograph all labeled or branded components.
- Identify the manufacturer and system name if possible.
- Photograph the installation from multiple angles showing how components connect.
- Note any visible deviations from a standard installation appearance.
- Do not apply generic pier-and-anchor evaluation criteria — flag for review against the specific manufacturer's instructions.

## Required observations summary

- Foundation type identified (or documented as unclear)
- Foundation material and condition
- Support spacing and support condition
- Anchorage presence, type, and condition
- Home-to-foundation connection method
- General site drainage condition
- Distress indicators: cracking, tilt, rotation, settlement, separation
- Moisture indicators: standing water, efflorescence, rot
- Accessory structures that may affect the engineering conclusion
- All inaccessible areas documented with reason

## Deliverables back to the engineer

- Complete labeled photo set organized by: exterior (each side), foundation type details, underside/crawlspace, connections, anchors, distress or deficiency shots
- Foundation type identified or flagged as unclear
- Short narrative summary addressing the engineer's specific questions
- Measured observations where requested
- Open issues that still prevent final certification
